\section{The problem: Clinicians with burnout} \label{Clinicians with burnout}
A well-documented current problem in medical practice is healthcare staff work stress that leads to burnout, i.e. physical and mental exhaustion. Burnout is a psychological syndrome whose symptoms comprise feeling emotionally exhausted and ineffective, experiencing impaired memory and attentiveness, finding work no longer meaningful, wanting to quit, and tending to view patients and colleagues as objects \cite{physicianburnout2018}. Clinical burnout goes back to at least 2016, since the proliferation of electronic health records (EHRs). Even though EHRs were introduced with the aim to eliminate the drawbacks of an old, paper-based system, they led to increased documentation workload, due to the time and energy placed onto the administrative task of capturing patient information and filling out intricate (i.e. non-intuitive) forms to prove compliance with government rules \cite{nejm2016burnout, physicianburnout2018}. For instance, a 2016 study estimated that about 27\% of physicians' time is allocated to direct clinical care, with the rest 73\% dissipating in administrative work \cite{timeallocation2016}. Other causes of burnout include limited time with patients, shift overload, and taking work at home. The consequences regarding patient care are many: poor communication, non-adherence to guidelines, medical errors, high organizational costs related to absenteeism and turnover, and legal costs due to complaints of negligence \cite{burnoutcovid2021}.

Especially for nurse personnel, a 2023 study showed a high risk of burnout and subsequent mental health concerns (depression, anxiety, suicidality) due to problems with understaffing, immense patient volumes -- in particular after the COVID-19 pandemic -- and exposure to traumatic events \cite{nursesburnout2024}.

Turning to the focus of this project, we now shift our attention to the modern psychiatric nurse, whose tasks include caring for, motivating, and supporting individuals suffering from mental health conditions or behavioral problems. The nurse's aim is to assist them patients to adapt their behavior to fit into society, in comparison to the more traditional goal of reactively managing the patients' symptoms \cite{understandingnursing2019}. A nurse's typical day-to-day duties include observing patients and evaluating their condition, administering, adjusting, and monitoring medications, educating patients about their illness, assisting with self-care (e.g. bathing, grooming, etc.), daily routines, and general physical health \footnote{'Psychiatric Nurse: Duties, Pay, and How to Become One'. November 29, 2023. Coursera. Accessed on October 28, 2024. \url{https://www.coursera.org/articles/psychiatric-nurse}}. Psychiatric nurses collaborate with doctors (psychiatrists and psychotherapists) to develop care plans and promote mental healthcare case by case, e.g. they may work with children exposed to trauma, or soldiers returning from the battlefield, among other cases. Psychiatric nurses may work in any setting that provides mental health services, such as correctional and assisted living facilities, rehabilitation centers, hospitals, clinics, and private homes. As the profession of psychiatry by nature deals with the unique psychological and emotional needs of vulnerable individuals -- where personalization of the interaction, building a trusting relationship, and showing compassion and empathy \footnote{`` In client-centered therapy, therapists might convey empathy through techniques such as active listening, reflection, and adopting a nonjudgmental stance and warm tone. Conversational agents might be able to model similar behaviors, at least through speech.`` \cite{artificialempathy2018}} are necessary skills -- the job of a psychiatric nurse can be very stressful \cite{understandingnursing2019}.

However, due to time pressures, the procedures are not always followed accurately, and information is not always immediately accessible. Of course, nurses have too little time to manually search for patient information on formal documents whenever necessary, and oftentimes they have to rely on their own memory for dozens of patients. As a result, the documentation part of their job diverts nurses' attention from the actual patient care part, leading to both poorer quality of care and increased mental burnout for the nurse staff. 

The solution to this may be the automation of information retrieval via an AI assistant.  The purpose of a conversational assistant would be to assist nurses to retrieve essential patient information with accuracy and timeliness. This should improve the staff’s well-being and job satisfaction, and free up their time to devote to patient care.


\section{The answer: Automation through AI} %Section - 1.2
In general, the main goal of leveraging AI in the healthcare domain is to improve the quality of care. In this particular case, this goal could be achieved by reducing the burden of documentation for nurses, thus reducing their cognitive load, de-stressing them, and redirecting their time and energy to focus on interactions with their patients. Language models could assist nurses by simplifying or even replacing everyday tasks that require text synthesis or analysis, such as drafting medical notes, reports, and administrative emails or letters, and summarizing dense patient medical records \cite{llmsmodernmedicine2023}. Ultimately, the goal is to help nurses focus on the outcomes, and not the procedures and protocols. The way this can be achieved is by leveraging the power of LLMs, which can read, summarize, and generate natural text.

Health professionals have already started using such solutions. As we can see from the image below, around two thirds of American doctors reported to have used Augmented Intelligence in their practice in 2024 for various tasks.

\subsection{Real-world healthcare data is unstructured}
In many healthcare settings, healthcare information is written fast, carelessly, and in an unstructured manner due to time pressures. Often this information is written with poor handwriting (if written manually), or typed without punctuation, and containing spelling errors. To address this challenge, AI-based solutions have emerged. Technologies such as Optical Character Recognition (OCR), e.g. \textit{Amazon Textract} \footnote{``Amazon Textract``, AWS (website), Accessed July 17, 2024.\url{https://aws.amazon.com/textract/}}, can digitize handwritten medical notes with increased accuracy. These technologies are then combined with natural language processing (NLP) models fine-tuned in the medical domain to correct spelling and punctuation errors, normalize abbreviations, and extract structured information from free-form text. Such systems can transform poorly typed (or written) input into usable, structured digital records, improving data usability.

\subsection{Hallucinations from LLMs} \label{hallucsLLM}
The unpredictability and non-deterministic nature of LLMs’ output is a major red flag for their use in medical practice, where even seemingly small mistakes may prove catastrophic, since they can put patient lives in danger. According to \citeauthor{opportunitiesrisks2024} ``these models can occasionally produce factually incorrect answers, make errors in simple arithmetic or logical problems, or generate responses that are seemingly obvious yet erroneous``. Specifically in healthcare, the use of the Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) system \textit{Whisper} that powers \textit{Nabla} -- an assistant for automated medical documentation used for millions of visits in the US, and by 30,000 clinicans -- has been recently reported to have introduced fabricated information into medical transcriptions, with even higher error rates for multilingual transcription and speech by aphasia patients \footnote{``RWS and BLEND Appoint New CEOs, Microsoft and Amazon Speech AI``, Slator (video), December 6, 2024. Accessed on December 7, 2024. \url{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Gvhf1rVZNGo}}.

To ensure AI safety, a solution is to employ professionals for AI red-teaming efforts, where the vulnerabilities of the system are challenged and exposed, with the aim of improving its weakest spots \cite{opportunitiesrisks2024}. 

Another solution is to use retrieval augmented generation (RAG), which is a method to take advantage of the parametric (embedded) memory and generative capabilities of an already trained LLM, while also greatly reducing its notorious hallucinations. The RAG architecture works by augmenting -- and also ``grounding`` -- the LLM via connecting it to an external corpus of information, i.e. a non-parametric (separate) memory storage. This corpus may be any type of data (PDF, spreadsheet, etc.), even proprietary or confidential. The way RAG works is that the LLM combines the user input (query) together with relevant documents, passages or facts retrieved from the corpus, in order to generate an accurate answer to the query \cite{raglewisetal2021}. In this way, the generated responses are based on factual context, bypassing the need to fine-tune and retrain the whole language model on numerous extra examples of domain-specific knowledge in order to achieve similar informed and current responses.

\subsection{Privacy of data and patient confidentiality}
Any information that can be used to trace an individual’s identity is called ``personally identifiable information`` (PII). The type of such information can be virtually anything, such as one's name, date of birth, passport number, driver’s license number, physical address, email address, photos, etc. Protected health information (PHI) is a subset of PII, but it specifically refers to health information acquired or generated during a health service, for example medical records, lab reports, discharge notes, and hospital bills. 

Various problems have arisen concerning medical data use for LLM training or deployment. For instance, the kind of patient data that will be uploaded and made available to an external LLM server to train an AI model, or the fact that many of the health data that might be used to adapt LLMs to medical contexts may fall under formal privacy protections (e.g. the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, or HIPAA). Another open question is what part of that training data would probably leak into the model's responses \cite{opportunitiesrisks2024}. Apart from the ethical and social aspect of disclosing confidential data about patients' mental and physical health (i.e. the individual may feel emotionally distressed and embarrassed, or they may face discrimination or harassment after their private records have been exposed publicly), security violations of private and sensitive patient records can be detrimental for both individuals and organizations from an economic and legal perspective, too. At the individual level, the damage may include identity theft, loss of money, extortion, or even physical harm. At the organizational level, the damage may include a loss of public trust, deteriorating reputation, criminal or civil penalties, or remediation costs to compensate the compromised individuals \cite{nist}.

To protect sensitive health information, the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services has enacted the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act Privacy Rule, which is a document that sets standards for the proper use and disclosure of PHI, how to store such information securely, and what to do if there is a breach. The Rule defines 18 items that mark health information as PHI \footnote{``HIPAA Administrative Simplification Regulation Text``, March 26, 2013, p. 97, Accessed January 28, 2024. \url{https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/hipaa-simplification-201303.pdf}}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Names
    \item Physical addresses (street address, city, county, zip code)
    \item Dates (including birth date, admission date, discharge date, and date of death)
    \item Phone numbers
    \item Fax numbers
    \item Email addresses
    \item Social Security numbers
    \item Medical record numbers
    \item Health plan beneficiary numbers
    \item Account numbers
    \item Certificate/license numbers
    \item Vehicle identification numbers (including license plate numbers)
    \item Device identifiers and serial numbers
    \item Web Universal Resource Locators (URLs)
    \item Internet Protocol (IP) address numbers
    \item Biometric identifiers (including fingerprints and voice)
    \item Full face photographic images and any comparable images
    \item Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code
\end{enumerate}

In order to align with ethical standards and societal norms, the solution is to follow specific guidelines and principles from the emerging field of Responsible AI for an ethical use of AI systems, including significant human oversight and impact assessments \cite{opportunitiesrisks2024}. Regarding training data specifically, a more practical approach is to de-identify, i.e. anonymize, PHI in order to prevent any potential linking of that data with any particular person. De-identification can be done via performing named entity recognition (NER) on clinical notes, in order to locate and then obscure the actual PHI information, e.g. names, dates, etc., by replacing it with a generic label, e.g. <NAME>, <DATE>, etc. An alternative for obscuring real PHI data is by replacing it with pseudo-random data, e.g. pseudonyms.

\subsection{(Mis)trust in AI and `alert fatigue`} \label{Mistrust}
There is still a substantial number of people who are more or less skeptical about the use of AI in healthcare. For example, there were relatively few applications of AI directly used in widespread clinical care as of the beginning of 2023, with some rare exceptions, even in medical imaging \cite{chaGPTpassesUSMLE2023}. ``One of the major challenges for technology in healthcare is that it needs to work well enough to drive widespread end-user adoption, demonstrate measurable time savings, and create a clear financial return on investment.`` \footnote{``Ambience Healthcare Raises \$70M to Scale the Most Comprehensive AI Operating System for Healthcare Organizations``, PR Newswire (press release), Accessed January 29, 2025. \url{https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/ambience-healthcare-raises-70m-to-scale-the-most-comprehensive-ai-operating-system-for-healthcare-organizations-302054056.html}}. Therefore, facing resistance from some nurse staff to full-fledged AI adoption in their work pipeline is to be expected. However, even tech-savvy nurses who otherwise trust AI in general, may resist the adoption of a conversational chatbot in their work. The reason may be attributed to the fact that healthcare employees are overworked already (see Section \ref{Clinicians with burnout}), and incorporating new ideas into existing workflows can be daunting and stress-inducing. A solution could be to make the use of the AI tool optional, and not mandatory, allowing nurses themselves to have agency on its use. With time, confidence in its use will grow, as with any other new tool.

\subsection{Informing patients about their condition and developing medical literacy} \label{SafetyRisks}
Comprehension of their condition has been found to empower patients, boost their satisfaction in regards to the medical care they are given, and cultivate a collaborative and trusting patient-clinician relationship \cite{simplifiedmednotes2020, patientsreaddocnotes2012}. Communicating the patient's health condition to them and to their family in a clear, understandable, non-jargon manner can help enhance the delivery of individualized and compassionate medical care \cite{chaGPTpassesUSMLE2023}. Clear communication has also been reported to improve compliance with medication regimens and treatment therapies, lessen patient anxiety, and improve clinical outcomes and quality of life, while decreasing treatment times and hospitalization costs \cite{patienteducation2006, educatepatientsandfam2014}. However, due to administrative and other time-consuming demands, physicians lack the time, the concentration or the willpower to engage in a genuine, detailed, and terminology-free conversation during a face-to-face patient visit \cite{educatepatientsandfam2014}. So, due to the clinical setting's pressures, physicians tend to intentionally refrain from explaining medical tests, results, or the pros and cons of a therapy, and even patients themselves hesitate to ask for information to be repeated \cite{BickmoreGiorgino2006}. There have been attempts to bridge this gap, for example via the well-known and successful \textit{OpenNotes} initiative, through which clinicians share medical notes with their patients \footnote{A 2020 study enhanced this process with external AI software that converted medical notes into plain language of 8th grade reading level \cite{simplifiedmednotes2020}. With this tool, clinicians did not need to change their note-taking style -- filled with abbreviations and medical jargon -- nor spend extra time to manually simplify or clarify their notes. Ultimately, patients judged the simplified notes to be desirable, useful, and empowering.}.

Nowadays patients have the means to know more about their condition, without being dependent on their physician's availability. It is now possible to simply input their doctors' notes or medical tests into any commercial, general-purpose AI chatbot and begin a conversation by ``Explain this to me in plain English``, or any other language \cite{llmsmodernmedicine2023}. The assistant can provide medical summarization, explanation of terminology, procedures, and medications, as well as general (i.e. not personalized) information on medical conditions, symptoms, causes, and treatments, based on publicly available reputable sources, such as MayoClinic or WebMD. For instance, some popular voice assistants are Google Assistant, Amazon Alexa, Apple Siri, and Samsung Bixby, and a common text-based assistant is ChatGPT. Such conversational AI systems, having absorbed a great amount of medical knowledge during their training, can perform tasks such as clinical reasoning, and answering medical questions quite accurately, even without fine-tuning \cite{chaGPTpassesUSMLE2023, gpt4israeli2024}. Since the early 2022 it has been established that language-based AI systems (e.g. ChatGPT running on GPT-3) can perform at such a level that they reach or exceed the 60\% passing grade for the US Medical Licensing Examination (i.e. the standardized exam used to license doctors), even with no specialized training input or prompting \cite{chaGPTpassesUSMLE2023}. Later in the same year it was found that GPT-4's performance had matured and was comparable to the median physician in all core medical disciplines of the Israeli board medical examinations (i.e. internal medicine, general surgery, pediatrics, psychiatry, and obstetrics and gynecology), with the exception of psychiatry, where it ranked \textit{higher} than the majority of physicians \cite{gpt4israeli2024}. GPT-4's performance reached or exceeded the passing rate levels in all five core medical domains, a fact which gives confidence in using the technology for clinical matters.

However, recent studies show that commercial, general-purpose, voice-based agents are neither always complete, safe nor communicatively suitable in their advice. In 2018, 30 health and lifestyle prompts were posed to \textit{Siri}, \textit{Google Assistant}, \textit{Alexa}, \textit{Bixby}, and \textit{Cortana}, which collectively reached only 41.0\% of appropriate responses to safety-critical prompts that needed a referral to a professional (e.g. depression,, heart attack), and 39\% to non-safety critical ones (i.e. physical health symptoms and lifestyle questions) \cite{healthprompts2020}. In this study, there were cases where the agents provided differing responses to the same prompts, or cases where they exhibited no sensitivity or empathy in their responses. Moreover, \citeauthor{mouth2019} have examined the speech recognition and comprehension performance of the top 50 US -- most of them complex -- medication names by popular conversational agents \textit{Siri}, \textit{Google Assistant}, and \textit{Alexa}, also from users with foreign accents of English. Comprehension accuracy for \textit{Google Assistant} was 91.8\%, for \textit{Siri} 58.5\%, and for \textit{Alexa} just 54.6\%, and notably the last 2 agents' performance deteriorated by around 10\% with a non-native English accent. The study raises concerns about the trustworthiness of responses based on misrecognized medication names. Further worrying results were reported by another 2018 study, which concluded that to rely on the aforementioned speech-based conversational assistants for actionable medical information, such as medication recommendations or emergency tasks, poses a safety risk for patients \cite{safetyrisks2018}. The study advises users to always consult health care professionals before following an AI response on any medical question. It is remarkable that, as mentioned before, the study participants were chosen to be only native users of English, because conversations with non-native users in a earlier pilot resulted in extremely high misrecognition rates.

\subsection{Promoting public health and behavior change} \label{PublicHealth}
Targeted conversational agents can play a decisive part in improving health literacy, health behaviors, and decision-making in the general population. They can be used as tools to encourage adults to increase and maintain their physical activity \cite{FitTrack2003, walkingincentives2020, exerciseandsun2018}, follow sun protection guidelines \cite{exerciseandsun2018}, manage eating disorders \cite{Tessa2022}, and improve contraceptive knowledge and behavior \cite{contraceptionreview2024}, or inform young people about alcohol, sex, and drug use \cite{youngedu2011}, and teach them healthy lifestyle strategies to ultimately build resilient minds \cite{healthylifestyleadolescents2024}. Even though they cannot replace human medical practitioners, they can act as a useful first point of contact, e.g. by doing initial screening and referring to a professional \cite{alcoholism2016} or as a virtual companion for positive behavior change \cite{FitTrack2003}. The upsides include confidentiality \cite{mentalhealthbarriers2017}, a wide range of trusted medical information, and the delivery of care irrespective of the user's location or time availability \cite{FitTrack2003, exerciseandsun2018, alcoholism2016}. Typically, they feature a friendly, mentor-style personality that establishes a close relation with the user \cite{FitTrack2003, alcoholism2016}.

On physical activity interventions for public health, \textit{Ally (Assistant to Lift your Level of activitY)} is a chat-based smartphone app used during a 2017 1.5-month study that promotes walking through delivering personalized planning and self-monitoring prompts \cite{walkingincentives2020}. The app tracks the user's steps and provides daily and weekly overviews of steps, distance, and energy consumed, offers a weekly planning of date, time and location of upcoming walks, and also sends reminders on the planned dates via the chatbot interface initiating a conversation with the user; the reminders include the daily step goal, the difference between the user’s current step count and the goal, an estimation of minutes needed to reach the goal, and an actionable tip on how to increase daily steps. The way \textit{Ally} sets the daily personalized step goal is based on the user’s past activity data and current average step count. Interestingly, the component that was linked with an 8.1\% greater probability of reaching daily step goals was the financial incentive, i.e. earning US\$ 1 per day for reaching the personalized step goal. A similar agent, \textit{FitTrack}, was designed in a 2003 study as an exercise advisor to help users achieve the recommended minimum level of daily physical activity of 10,000 steps or 30 minutes of activity while maintaining a social relationship with them \cite{FitTrack2003}. The embodied \footnote{ ``Embodied conversational agents are animated humanoid computer-based characters that use speech, eye gaze, hand gesture, facial expression, and other nonverbal modalities to emulate the experience of human face-to-face conversation with their user``. \cite{cassell2001embodied}} computer-based agent used synthesized speech and synchronized nonverbal behavior (hand gestures, body posture shifts, gazing at and away from the user, raising and lowering eyebrows, head nods, and walking on and off the screen) to build emotional connection. Users interacted in a constrained manner, i.e. by selecting items from multiple-choice menus of text phrases, which were dynamically updated based on the conversational context. They received standard behavioral interventions, such as self-monitoring (progress charts showing their activity levels over time), and educational content on the topic of walking for exercise. They also held a daily conversation with the agent about their progress and any obstacles they had regarding exercising, and were offered tips to overcome them. A similarly informative, interactive, and engaging agent was developed in 2012 for a 12-month clinical trial to promote and maintain positive behavior change regarding physical activity and ultraviolet light exposure protection, in order to preempt certain types of cancer, e.g. skin cancer \cite{exerciseandsun2018}. While conversing with the participants, the agent provided answers, support, and encouragement, and appeared empathetic and attentive to them thanks to its non-verbal (intonation, human-like mannerisms, etc.), and social dialogue skills (humor, storytelling, small talk, etc.). The agent also emailed periodic reminders to motivate consistent physical exercise and sun protection behaviors, and it eventually achieved an 88\% user satisfaction, and a 77\% behavior change.

A few text-based assistants have also been developed to manage distorted weight and shape concerns of people who suffer from or are at high risk of eating disorders (EDs) \cite{Tessa2022}. One of them, called \textit{Tessa}, was designed in 2022 to target young adult women at risk of developing EDs by delivering ``Body Positive``, an EDs prevention program. The program comprised the following sessions: challenging the thin-body ideal, media literacy, healthy eating, critical comments, exercise, bingeing, and maintenance, all through synchronous free-form conversation.

Chatbots have also been developed to aid family planning by providing fertility as well as contraceptive information, and linkages to products and services to adults of reproductive age \cite{contraceptionreview2024}. However, there are conflicting results on their impact, i.e. on the increase in contraceptive knowledge and on the intention of users to actually use contraception.

As we will see in a subsequent Section (\ref{Chronic disease}), there exist conversational agents that promote understanding of asthma \cite{max2021}, and various types of cancer \cite{vik2020}, which is fundamental to self-management and better disease control.

On alcohol abuse, a 2016 study on young adults examined a text-based chatbot intervention that provided education on responsible alcohol use, and  performed a standardized risk assessment of the user's drinking habits \cite{alcoholism2016}. The chatbot gave relevant feedback, advice, and links to government agencies and private organizations for more information on how to seek medical assistance, if necessary.

On sexual education, many young people refrain from getting medical advice, and therefore a diagnosis and medication, for fear of stigma, discrimination and homophobic attitudes, thus remaining unaware of their status and endangering their partners' health \cite{shihbot2017}. \textit{SHIHbot} was developed to educate on HIV and AIDS disseminating reliable, accurate information. An earlier chatbot was developed in 2011 by \cite{youngedu2011} to give answers to questions about sex, alcohol, and drug use to high school students, who do not often get adequate information from their parents or school. The goal was to help adolescents make informed decisions before experiencing these risky behaviors themselves. The chatbot was used extensively, and was perceived to be faster, more concise, and more anonymous than typical search engines.

Keeping to young people, a recent 2020-2021 intervention of 12 weeks was designed to decrease sedentary behaviors, and increase physical activity, sleep quality, and breakfast frequency for 12-15 y.o. school students with the aim of promoting general well-being and mental resilience \cite{healthylifestyleadolescents2024}. The mobile-based health app, called \textit{\#LIFEGOALS}, had several components, as it comprised an activity and sleep tracker, self-regulation techniques (goal-setting, coping strategies, self-monitoring, etc.), gamification elements (earning virtual coins after completing goals to personalize the app's  avatar), weekly health-related narrative video episodes, and a chatbot for answering questions on lifestyle behaviors, and sending encouraging messages and timely reminders for engaging with the app. The intervention had positive outcomes on learning about and adopting good lifestyle habits; however, the COVID-19 pandemic ``shelter-at-home`` restriction measures moderated some of these.

On sleep, a 2021 research study developed a dialogue system that acts as a sleep coach, aiming to provide relevant information, and make a diagnosis on all potential behavioral causes of sleep issues. Finally, the system recommends possible next steps, directing users to helpful lifestyle changes, such as limiting caffeine and alcohol consumption before bedtime.


\subsection{Checking personalized symptoms and offering a diagnosis} \label{Diagnosis}
Diagnosing patients means classifying them into the most fitting class of disease, via examining their clinical status. This is why for a proper diagnosis a clinician needs to gather accurate symptoms. In order to so, face-to-face, meaningful, and lengthy clinical interviews are very important for communicating empathy and eliciting patient trust. However, problems such as the limited visit time, the patient's geographical location, their hesitancy to share intimate information with another human \cite{mdddiagnosis2017, willingtodisclose2014, machinesinmentalhealth2017, mentalhealthbarriers2017}, their fear of stigmatization \cite{mentalhealthbarriers2017}, or diverging from the standardized procedure on the side of the physician can harm a diagnosis, especially of mental health disorders \cite{mdddiagnosis2017}. Therefore, the benefits of safe, ethical, and robust diagnostic conversational agents can be many: access to primary care even to under-served populations \cite{eds2014}, fewer misdiagnoses as well as earlier diagnoses that lead to better treatment of diseases and recovery rates \cite{mdddiagnosis2017}, ``humanized`` anonymity and higher patient satisfaction \cite{mdddiagnosis2017, mentalhealthbarriers2017, vickybot2023}, and cost reduction \cite{mdddiagnosis2017, AMIE2024}. Such agents could act as a decision aid to both patients and physicians: ``AI systems capable of such diagnostic dialogues could increase availability, accessibility, quality and consistency of care by being useful conversational partners to clinicians and patients alike.`` \footnote{Alan Karthikesalingam and Vivek Natarajan, 'AMIE: A research AI system for diagnostic medical reasoning and conversations', Google Research (blog), January 12, 2024. Accessed on September 3, 2024, https://research.google/blog/amie-a-research-ai-system-for-diagnostic-medical-reasoning-and-conversations/}

Google's \textit{AMIE (Articulate Medical Intelligent Explorer)} is an AI agent based on a medical LLM that enables natural, synchronous, and text-based conversation with patients with the aim of offering an accurate diagnosis. AMIE is an impressive tool for the field of clinical reasoning, not only because it can maintain fluent and empathetic conversations, but also because it actively elicits missing information through the conversation.
Its training is novel: through self-play in the roles of doctor and patient, chain-of-reasoning, and automated feedback mechanisms it has learnt to evaluate medical history and symptoms with high precision. \textit{AMIE} has been trained on datasets of real-world medical reasoning, summaries, and clinical conversations, but also on simulated conversations.
\textit{AMIE} was tested on text-based dialogue by simulating consultations with trained actors, and was evaluated on areas of consultation quality, such as history-taking completeness, diagnostic accuracy, management plan, and communication skills. The system was rated superior to human primary care physicians in 28 out of 32 areas from the perspective of specialist physicians, and in 24 out of 26 areas from the perspective of patient actors \cite{AMIE2024}. As for its conversational performance, \textit{AMIE} scored higher than human physicians in communication and empathy, and was reported to have longer and more structured replies. This conclusion is consistent with a prior study where chatbot (ChatGPT) responses to health questions posted by patients on a social media forum (cf. Section above) were rated as more preferable, and significantly more empathetic, more detailed, and of higher quality than the responses from actual, verified clinicians \cite{ayersetall2023redditempathy}.

Older initiatives include ``virtual humans`` programmed to follow standardized diagnostic procedures to help the professional clinician with automating the diagnosis procedure. For sleep disorders, and especially the common condition of excessive daytime sleepiness, a voice-based, embodied conversational agent was developed for a 2014 study to screen patients and offer an accurate diagnosis, with the aim of helping physicians. Indeed, very few are trained to adequately differentiate sleeping disorders from fatigue or even psychiatric conditions, resulting in prescribing the wrong treatment \cite{eds2014}. The agent was programmed to conduct a simple interview based on the widely used Epworth Sleepiness Scale, which is a self-administered, standardized questionnaire. After scoring a 80\% for diagnosing alert patients, and 98\% on diagnosing the sleepiest ones, it showed that a virtual physician can assist real ones in diagnosing patients with sleeping disorders; it was also well received by patients.

Similarly, an embodied conversational agent was developed in 2015 to identify specific symptoms and diagnose major depressive disorder in the general population via a face-to-face interview, following a list of criteria of a highly structured, reliable, and validated procedure \cite{mdddiagnosis2017}. Patients rated the agent as trustworthy, commended on its ability to communicate empathy, and also noted the agent helped reduce their feeling of being judged by a human interviewer. The agent efficiently identified patients without depression and also those with severe depression. Another chatbot, named \textit{Vickybot}, was developed in a 2022 study to self-assess, monitor, and deliver psychological treatment modules according to severity (e.g. mindfulness) for depression, anxiety, and work-related burnout, and for detecting suicidal risk. The smartphone-based app was useful in diagnosing and significantly reducing occupational burnout (but not depressive and anxiety symptoms) and in intervening in emergency situations (e.g. suicidal thoughts and behaviors) by immediately providing emergency resources and alerting the research team \cite{vickybot2023}.

On PTSD screening, two interventions have been studied specifically for military service members, who are much more likely to have such disorder compared to civilians. First, \citeauthor{mentalhealthbarriers2017} developed a 2017 virtual human interviewer that establishes an anonymous as well as rapport-building interaction via verbal and non-verbal channels, e.g. utterances of agreements, head nods, etc. The virtual interviewer was highly accepted, and was found to increase accurate disclosure of PTSD and mental distress symptoms, compared to a traditional assessment. Also, \textit{SimCoach}, another virtual human that uses conversational speech and gestures, was designed in 2015 to encourage military service members, especially those with signs or symptoms of PTSD or depression, to seek help to improve their psychological health \cite{SimCoach2016}. The intervention offered voice-based screening for assessing psychological health through a symptom checklist, and conversational dialogue through which users' entries signifying distress would trigger personalized recommendations, e.g. direction to web pages that mention treatment or help-seeking services. However, there was no benefit regarding the participants' intent to seek help, according to the study's evaluation. 

On alcoholism, as described in Section \ref{PublicHealth}, \citeauthor{alcoholism2016} have studied a text-based chatbot intervention that performed a screening of risky drinking habits, and gave relevant advice and links to government agencies and private organizations for more information on how to seek medical assistance, if necessary.

A much earlier mixed-initiative and voice-based conversational agent, \textit{ERA}, was used in a 2006 study for decision support to general practitioners and nurse practitioners to help determine whether a patient should be referred urgently to a cancer specialist for suspected cancer \cite{decisioncancer2006}. The system was designed to be used over the phone to acquire data regarding the patient’s signs and symptoms, while also re-phrasing and elaborating a question, asking clarification questions on the user's responses, and explaining its reasoning; the final decision is made by the user.

In the domain of clinical decision support, treatment selection after a diagnosis is also a field that has been examined for conversational agents. A preliminary study of 2023 examined medication recommendations by ChatGPT-plus (GPT-4 model) regarding prescriptions of anti-depressant drugs for major depression after initial antidepressant nonresponse \cite{Perlis2023}. The model identified optimal treatment for 76\% of test cases, but also advised less optimal or contraindicated approaches (e.g. prescribing medication associated with weight gain to obese patients) for almost half of them. Interestingly, the model provided as rationale for treatment selection a number of simple heuristics commonly applied by human physicians in psychopharmacologic decision-making to better match patients with optimal treatments, such as avoidance of prior non-responsive medications and avoidance of medications with adverse effects based on medical comorbidities. A follow-up study of 2024 examined next-step treatment options for bipolar disorder, where the GPT-4 model was augmented with recent bipolar treatment guidelines within each prompt \cite{perlisetal2024}. The model was asked to identify 5 optimal next-step pharmacotherapies and 5 poor or contraindicated choices, and explain its rationale. For 94.9\% of test cases the optimal treatment was among the top 5 selected by the model, and for 84.4\% the optimal treatment was among the top 3. Only for 12\% of cases did the model select a medication considered by the experts to be a poor choice or contraindicated. Both studies are encouraging observations for future applications of such models as interpretable tools for guiding prescriptions in other clinical domains, too.

An important, recent study from October 2024 by \citeauthor{DiagnosticReasoningLLM2024} indicated that a commercially available chatbot (ChatGPT Plus, GPT-4) could outperform generalist physicians with a range of experience levels in making accurate clinical diagnoses. Interestingly, the chatbot alone was found to perform significantly better in diagnosing the given -- albeit curated and summarized -- patient cases in comparison to the two human groups: physicians with access to the same LLM, and physicians with access to conventional resources. The study demonstrates that, for human physicians, access to generative LLMs will not automatically improve diagnostic reasoning; they will need targeted training to integrate these resources appropriately into their workflow (e.g. in prompt engineering techniques).

\subsection{Empowering medical students}
Using generative AI in healthcare can have a lot of advantages in regards to the clinical, but also the educational setting, especially the domain of medical education. AI-generated answers have been found to bring along significant and novel insights, coupled with a deductive reasoning process \cite{chaGPTpassesUSMLE2023}. What is more, conversational agents like \textit{AMIE} can act as an an educational tool for medical students, and embodied conversational agents can be used as virtual patients for training in detecting certain conditions, e.g. suicidal behavior \cite{ecaforsuicide2017}.


\subsection{Monitoring post-treatment plans and supporting self-management for chronic diseases} \label{Chronic disease}
People with chronic diseases, like cancer, hypertension, diabetes, chronic pain, etc., can benefit from AI companions in keeping track of their ongoing medication routines and monitoring their symptoms \cite{CAsinhealthsurvey2019, vik2020, asthma2014, machinesinmentalhealth2017, max2021, GoogleHomediabetes2018}, gaining more knowledge and self-confidence in managing their disease \cite{asthma2014, stress2017}, and even getting personalized feedback from their physicians \cite{max2021}. Specifically, self-management of a condition comprises adherence to a certain medication, activity and diet plan, as well as lifestyle changes to eliminate long-held dysfunctional behaviors and prevent relapses \cite{hudlicka2013, stress2017, selma2020}. Also, using AI agents instead of paper records to check with chronic patients can promote more accurate and complete recordings, while saving time for health care providers \cite{17thIEEESymposium2004, childhoodobesity2017, tensiobot2021} and reducing the health systems burden in general \cite{mydiabetescoach2020}. Scheduling and managing medical appointments is another feature of medical agents that helps towards reducing missed appointments, and prevents patients from forgetting to bring their record sheets to their health centre, as they are carried within the app \cite{tensiobot2021}. From a social perspective, conversational agents can coordinate the communication among health care professionals, family members, and patients, and achieve a holistic support network, while saving patients from visiting hospitals for on-site consultations \cite{childhoodobesity2017, max2021}. Specifically for chronic pain (e.g. back pain due to accident, surgery, physical decline, etc.), where cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is the standard therapy, there exist significant barriers to its management, e.g. a lack of adequately qualified providers, an inconvenient distance regarding the location of the few qualified ones, and a lack of time and money for on-site proper consultations \cite{selma2020}; there are similar challenges connected to chronic stress management \cite{hudlicka2013, stress2017}. For all the above, interventions via conversational agents can be a cost-effective, accessible and flexible solution.

A 2019 study demonstrated that conversational agents can improve medication adherence rates of patients with breast cancer as well as their quality of life in general \cite{vik2019}. \textit{Vik}, a text-based chatbot, was designed to empower patients with breast cancer and their relatives, by responding to their queries and concerns with quality-checked medical information, and reminding them of their prescription. The study analyzed the usage data of patients conversing with \textit{Vik} for a year, showing that the more the patients used the chatbot, the more adherent they were. Further, \textit{Vik}'s answers to common questions asked by breast cancer patients about their therapy management were found to be non-inferior and as satisfactory as information by a multidisciplinary committee (medical, radiation, and surgical oncologists) \cite{vik2019b}. Actually, there exist many \textit{Viks}, each designed to provide quality-checked information about the treatment, side-effects, quality of life, reimbursement and patients rights for other cancers and chronic diseases, such as headaches, asthma, etc. \cite{vik2020}. 

Turning to hypertension, however, two clinical trials have shown no improvement in home management compared to a purely blood pressure tracking app \cite{rcthypertension2020} or compared to pen-and-paper \cite{tensiobot2021}. Specifically, the text-based smartphone app \textit{HPCP} (Hypertension Personal Control Program) app was developed to support users on hypertension self-management and coach them on healthy behaviors including medication adherence, blood pressure (BP) measurement, diet, physical activity, sleep, and stress management. For example, to promote home monitoring \textit{HPCP} encouraged, reminded, and tracked blood pressure measurements regularly, and recommended retaking readings or contacting a medical professional in case of outlier or extreme values. Similarly, the text-based \textit{TensioBot} was a chatbot operating on Telegram and designed for assisting patients and physicians with blood pressure home measurements. Its features include alerts for measurements, notifications of data recording errors, video- and text-based advice on good BP measurement practices, reminders and rescheduling of upcoming medical appointments. The trial confirmed that chatbot reminders do not affect treatment adherence (i.e. BP check at a fixed schedule) or number of BP checkings. However, it is important to note that chatbot users scored higher in knowledge and skills regarding home BP measurement technique, and that the bot assisted doctors and nurses by informing them accurately about BP values, thanks to a downloadable report sheet.

Turning to asthma, improved self-management and treatment adherence was reported in two studies targeting adolescent patients \cite{asthma2014, max2021}, where parents greatly appreciated the deeper understanding and empathy towards the previously unknown degree of afflictions their children were facing. Despite being one of the earliest chatbots (2014), the text-based \textit{mASMAA} (mobile phone-based Asthma Self-Management Aid for Adolescents) was used to interact with teenagers in free-form text and facilitate symptom monitoring, increase awareness of symptoms and triggers, support treatment adherence, give a sense of control over the disease, and promote adolescent–parent partnership via emailing daily reports to parents. Conversations between patients and \textit{mASMAA} included questions and answers about asthma and its triggers, interpretation of the patients' medical records, system-initiated messages to help them monitor symptoms (through automated daily asthma-monitoring inquiries) as well as activity levels, and reminders to take medications according to the patients' individual schedule. A similar study of 2021 showed improved cognitive and behavioral skills after the agentic intervention of \textit{MAX}, a text-based conversational agent aiming to help asthmatic young patients to adopt healthy behaviors (i.e., inhalation technique) as well as gain knowledge about their condition \cite{max2021}. \textit{MAX} took the concept of virtual asthma coach one step further by engaging the complete triangle of stakeholders: health care professionals (via email), patients (via the app), and family members (via SMS text messaging), thereby establishing a robust working alliance acting as a mediator. For instance, parents would record a video of their children's inhalation procedure, upload it on a secure server, and then \textit{MAX} would notify the physician for review and feedback, which would then be sent to the child through the app. What is more, it also introduced gamification for patients, so as to increase their perceived enjoyment and understanding, by introducing the point system after successful asthma quizzes, and for each finished coaching session.

About monitoring chronic pain, the Pain Monitoring Voice Diary was implemented in 2006 for real-time assessment of experience and behavior in the patient’s natural environment \cite{painmonitoring2006}. This electronic data collection technique allowed for real-time data collection of information from patients over the phone, as they were responding to spoken dialogue system prompts using unconstrained speech. A more recent implementation is \textit{SELMA}, a text-based smartphone chatbot designed and implemented in a 2018 clinical trial to deliver psychoeducation (via cognitive behavior therapy) for pain management \cite{selma2020}. Psychoeducation enhances peoples’ understanding of their disease and their self-help motivation, while reducing their insecurity. The pain self-management coach guides patients through CBT modules that addresses dysfunctional cognitions and emotions, and teaches coping strategies with the aim to control pain. What is more, it provides relevant exercises, such as on stress release and mindfulness, and follow-up questions for the participants. Nevertheless, comparison of the intervention and control groups showed similar effects in therms of pain-related impairment, general well-being, and pain intensity.

On chronic stress management, a 2017 clinical trial showed that a conversational agent in combination with medical group visits was able to reduce long-term stress -- the primary symptom for patients with chronic pain, depression, and anxiety -- by motivating adherence to positive behaviors and providing personalized guidance \cite{stress2017}. \textit{Gabby}, the voice-based animated conversational agent, was designed for patients to use at home for reviewing the educational material covered in more detail, as well as for practising self-care skills, such as meditation, self-massage, and yoga based on the widely-used training program 'Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction' (MBSR). \textit{Gabby} could express herself with eyebrow movements, hand gestures, and gaze and posture shifts, apart from having a natural, calming speech, and received user input via multiple choice selection of utterances on the screen. An earlier intervention, the embodied \textit{Virtual Mindfulness Coach} prototype, was developed in 2013 to provide meditation coaching via mixed initiative, text-based, free-form conversation enhanced with multiple-choice based input for reduction of stress, pain, and anxiety disorder's symptoms as well as for prevention of relapse into depression \cite{hudlicka2013}. The coach, a female character named \textit{Chris}, showed a variety of facial expressions, gestures, and gazes, which she adjusted reacting with empathy to the user's input and emotional state. \textit{Chris} offered customized guidance on the lesson materials of MBSR curriculum, prviding encouragement, advice and additional information, correcting misconceptions, and asking follow-up questions on specific content based on the previous dialog and the user's concerns (e.g., “Is mindfulness good for stress?” “Will meditating help me feel less stressed?”). She was demonstrated to help users establish a more frequent and longer mindfulness practice, compared to a self-administered program via written and audio materials.

On diabetes self-management, the current state of the art mostly assists patients via mobile applications, like \textit{Care4Life Diabetes} \footnote{Care4Life, Southern Nevada Health District, YouTube (video), Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y1VxFZkajGg}} or \textit{MyDiabetesConnect} \footnote{Diabetes Connect LTD, Google Play, YouTube (video), Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.mydiabetesconnect&hl=en}}. These applications offer a dashboard for diabetic-related data points, recipes for diabetics, exercise and weight monitoring, customizable reminders for checking glucose levels as well as for taking medications and not missing doctor appointments, blood glucose and blood pressure tracking and summary reports, and educational messages \cite{care4life2021}. Some apps include encouraging messages \footnote{BEAT Diabetes, Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://www.beatdiabetes.org/}}, a comprehensive diabetes management education plan \footnote{Glucose Buddy, Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://www.glucosebuddy.com/}}, the ability to connect the glucose meter with the app for error-free logging \footnote{mySugr, Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://www.mysugr.com/en-us}}, downloadable statistics and charts  \footnote{Diabetes:M, Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://diabetes-m.com/features/}}, the ability to upload photos of meals to log nutrition information \footnote{Undermyfork, Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://www.undermyfork.com/}}, pairing with a smartwatch to display glucose levels \footnote{Gluroo, Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://gluroo.com/}}, sharing real-time data to the whole family's smartphones, and also a synced web platform for healthcare professionals to view their patients' logs \footnote{Health 2 Sync, Accessed on September 25, 2024. \url{https://www.health2sync.com/careproviders}}. However, inputting information on a screen can be difficult and even frustrating for elderly patients. \textit{Healthy Coping} is a voice-based application that assists the older generation with self-management of type 2 diabetes by combining the Google Home's device voice interface for hosting the conversational agent, and a paired web interface for a visual display of the patient's monitoring results, intended for both the patients themselves and their physicians. It focuses on monitoring blood glucose levels, taking medication, being active, eating a healthy diabetic diet, and mental health coping (via offering a depression screening survey). It also offers personalized advice for maintaining target glucose levels, and reminders for taking medication, all through intuitive voice commands or questions expressed in natural language (e.g. “I want to take the coping survey.”, “I want to monitor my health”, “What should I eat?”) \cite{GoogleHomediabetes2018}. It is notable that this dialogue system design is not innovative compared to much earlier telemedicine and phone monitoring solutions, such as \textit{DI@L-log} from 2005 \cite{18thIEEESymposium2005}. These solutions were used for data collection and for offering encouraging feedback and advice to patients through a voice user interface, and also supplied physicians with progress charts of weight, blood glucose values, etc. as well as alerts for deviant readings through a graphical user interface \cite{17thIEEESymposium2004}.

On childhood obesity, a 2017 5-month study implemented a text-based smartphone app that monitored and supported young patients during their treatment, and assisted their health carers too \cite{childhoodobesity2017}. For rapport building there were a female or male persona to select, use of the teenager's first name, use of informal language and emoticons, engaging in small-talk, and expressing empathy. This assistant was programmed to offer a challenge a day, e.g. a relaxation technique for stress management, a step counter for physical activity, photographing meals for diet adherence, and answering entertaining quiz questions for promoting health literacy. The results of the challenges were reviewed in the next consultation hour.

Interestingly, attitudes of long-term patients on healthcare chatbots vary. A survey study conducted between January and March 2023 reported AI hesitancy among such population: they were skeptical about the use of AI chatbots for health care purposes, and less than 30\% were likely to use a health chatbot in the following 12 months due to mistrust in its accuracy \cite{aihesitancy2024}. In the survey, chronic patients seemed more receptive to using voice-based chatbots for nutrition, sleep, and mental well-being, i.e. stress and emotional state \cite{aihesitancy2024}. Contrary results have been reported for the cancer agent \textit{Vik}, whose users expressed an overall satisfaction at a 94\% and even recommended the chatbot to their friends \cite{vik2019}. Also, a large majority of experiment participants have commended on the chatbot's ease of use and usefulness for the asthma agents \textit{mASMAA}  and \textit{MAX}, and the hypertension agent \textit{TensioBot} \cite{asthma2014, max2021, selma2020}. Specifically for \textit{TensioBot}, most patients indicated they prefer to register their BP at home using \textit{TensioBot} instead of manually; additionally, 85\% continued its use even after the experiment was over. 


\subsection{Managing mental health conditions and providing behavioral counseling} \label{MentalHealthRisks}
Mental illnesses are very common: it has been estimated that 46\% Europeans experienced an emotional or psychosocial problem during 2022 \footnote{European Commission. Mental health. Accessed on October 8, 2024. \url{https://health.ec.europa.eu/non-communicable-diseases/mental-health_en}}. Also 1 in 5 adults in the U.S. live with mental health \cite{nami_mental_health_numbers}, and 1 in 8 people worldwide \footnote{Mental health worldwide - Statistics \& Facts. Statista. Accessed on October 8, 2024. \url{https://www.statista.com/topics/8066/mental-health-worldwide/}}. Some mental health issues are chronic, non-curable, and can only be managed by medication, therapy, and lifestyle changes throughout the patient's life, in the same way other chronic health conditions described in Section \ref{Chronic disease} are managed. Such mental illnesses include schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, certain types of depression, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), and Substance Use Disorders (SUDs). Some others are non-chronic and treatable with medication and therapy, e.g. mild-to-moderate depression, Acute Stress Disorder (ASD), panic disorder (in some cases), and phobias. Unfortunately, less than 1 in 3 people get mental health care worldwide \footnote{WHO’s Science in 5: Mental Health - 1 July 2022, Accessed on October 8, 2024. \url{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lOEXhd6c5k0}}. Untreated mental health conditions lead to high healthcare costs, impaired functioning and loss of autonomy of the patient in everyday life and in the professional domain, and social isolation \cite{schizophrenia2010, dementiatherapy2019, panicbot2020}. Access to therapies that can manage or even cure a mental health disorder is often hindered by shortages in mental healthcare professionals \cite{dementiatherapy2019, aipsychotherapy2019, depressioncoach2023, wysa2023, stade2024}, high costs for the individual \cite{depressioncoach2023, machinesinmentalhealth2017} and lack of time for frequent on-site consultations on the patients' side \cite{bickmoreetalrealtionalfordepression2010, dementiatherapy2019}, as well as avoidance of seeking support in-person through formal mental health services, or of giving an honest reporting of symptoms during official screenings, due to a variety of reasons: fear of social stigma \cite{schizophrenia2010, onlinementalhealth2015, alcoholism2016, woebot2017, mentalhealthbarriers2017, bickmoreetalrealtionalfordepression2010, mentalhealthandviolence2016, noblesetal2020addiction}, judgment and discrimination \cite{mentalhealthbarriers2017}, risk of blocking career advancement \cite{SimCoach2016}, reluctance to disclose intense or negative emotions and behaviors to other humans \cite{bickmoreetalrealtionalfordepression2010, willingtodisclose2014, machinesinmentalhealth2017, mdddiagnosis2017}, and impairments in emotions and communication due the illness itself \cite{schizophrenia2010}. 
Thankfully, conversational agents can be an affordable, accessible, acceptable by users, and reliable solution in providing 24/7 remote psychoeducation \footnote{   
``Psychoeducation, the goal of which is to help people better understand (and become accustomed to living with) mental health conditions, is considered to be an essential aspect of all therapy programs. It is generally known that those who have a thorough understanding of the challenges they are facing as well as knowledge of personal coping ability, internal and external resources, and their own areas of strength are often better able to address difficulties, feel more in control of the condition(s), and have a greater internal capacity to work toward mental and emotional well-being.``, GoodTherapy (blog). Accessed on October 19, 2024. \url{https://www.goodtherapy.org/blog/psychpedia/psychoeducation}} \cite{schizophrenia2010, panicbot2020, adhd2021}, therapy \cite{dementiatherapy2019, woebot2017, machinesinmentalhealth2017, depressioncoach2023, panicbot2020, adhd2021} and mental health assessment to those who need it \cite{SimCoach2016, mentalhealthbarriers2017, mdddiagnosis2017}, while simulating the interpersonal relationship of a mental health care provider \cite{schizophrenia2010, mentalhealthbarriers2017}, securing anonymity \cite{mentalhealthbarriers2017, wysa2023}, and providing unbiased recommendations \cite{alcoholism2016} as well as psychological support and information immediately, anytime, and anywhere \cite{schizophrenia2010, reviewmental2017, woebot2017, machinesinmentalhealth2017, noblesetal2020addiction, depressioncoach2023, smokingcessation2017, smokingcessation2019}. In this way, medication and exercise adherence can be increased, and hospital re-admissions as well as symptoms can be reduced \cite{schizophrenia2010}. A systematic review on the feasibility and effectiveness of interventions based on one-on-one, synchronous, written conversations has shown significant and sustained improvements in mental health outcomes, which are even equivalent (not superior, though) to face-to-face therapy and telephone counseling \cite{reviewmental2017}. Last, let us not forget that the first well-known conversational chatbot, \textit{ELIZA}, was programmed back in 1966 to simulate a text-based conversation with a psychotherapist \cite{eliza1966}.

On depression and anxiety, the use of generative AI chatbots has recently increased, and research has indicated promising results in their use for meaningful mental health support. Two recent 2024 pilot studies used ChatGPT as a support tool in psychiatric care, albeit with a small sample size (12 and 19 patients, respectively) \cite{melo2024chatgpt, siddals2024}. The chatbot offered effective and accessible mental health care in the form of self-help advice, relationship coaching, psychoeducation, and emotional support, and scored high in both patient engagement and satisfaction. It also showed a substantial improvement in self-perceived quality of life, such as better interpersonal relationships and mood, and healing from trauma and loss. However, despite the overall positive feedback, a few participants complained about the irrelevant or overly long responses, and the restrictive safety guardrails that often urged users to contact a human professional, discontinuing the conversation. Moving to rule-based bots, in a 2023 pilot trial a virtual, voice-based coach, \textit{Lumen}, offered problem-solving treatment (PST) therapy to adults with mild-to-moderate depression and/or anxiety \cite{depressioncoach2023}. The PST coach acts as a guide to identify a problem, set a goal, brainstorm solutions, choose a solution, develop an action plan, and to implement and evaluate the plan. After 4 months with \textit{Lumen}, self-reported rational problem-solving, impulsivity scores as well as total psychological distress showed meaningful improvements. Six years earlier, a text-based conversational agent, named \textit{Woebot}, was developed in study to deliver a 2-week self-help CBT program for young adults with symptoms of anxiety and depression with great success in reducing these symptoms and in keeping the users engaged nearly every day \cite{woebot2017}. The reasons for its success were attributed to \textit{Woebot's} interactional skills: use of emojis to express affect, showing empathy on users' responses, checking in and following up on users' mood, and sending a personalized message every day to initiate a conversation. The bot also offered weekly charts depicting each participant’s mood over time, accompanied by a brief summary to facilitate reflection. Another text-based intervention was implemented in 2015 with \textit{TeenChat}, designed to listen, comfort, and encourage stressed out young adults to practise coping strategies, as a ``virtual friend`` would, with the aim of releasing stress and avoiding deterioration inside the young and immature minds (e.g. depression, suicide) \cite{TeenChat2015}. Turning to launched products, a popular mental health app is \textit{Wysa} \footnote{Wysa (website). Accessed on October 13, 2024. \url{https://www.wysa.com/}}, which is a text-based coach designed to help its users deal with anxiety, worry, and low mood by teaching skills to manage their emotional resilience, while ensuring their anonymity. \textit{Wysa} uses CBT, meditation, breathing, yoga, and motivational interviewing to help build mental skills in a self-help context; it also offers direct access to one-on-one sessions with mental health professionals. Also, a 2020-2021 study on users' engagement with \textit{Wysa} during the COVID-19 pandemic confirmed that conversing with the app can result in a significant reduction in symptoms of anxiety and depression, further suggesting that digital health interventions can provide large populations with efficient support for mental health care \cite{wysa2023}. A similar, text-based conversational assistant that offers mental health assessment and one-on-one counseling on a wide range of mental health and well-being issues, such as depression, anxiety, work stress, grief, eating disorders, bullying, addiction, etc., is \textit{Noni} \footnote{7cups (website). Accessed on October 19, 2024. \url{https://www.7cups.com/}}. \textit{Noni} also offers the opportunity to chat live with a volunteer listener, take part in guided chat-based group therapy with other users, and connect with human therapists via videocalls.

Panic disorder, which falls under anxiety disorders, is characterized by recurrent and unexpected panic attacks, i.e. sudden surges of intense fear or discomfort. A 2020 clinical trial developed a mobile, text-based app, named \textit{Todaki}, to relieve panic symptoms, such as social anxiety, rapid heart rate, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, or a feeling of losing control \cite{panicbot2020}. \textit{Todaki} offers CBT training to correct distorted thinking, as well as reliable information on the disorder which it tests with quizzes in every conversation, it describes the effects and side-effects of medication, it shows videos of psychiatrists for breathing and muscle relaxation training, it suggests coping skills for real-time symptom management, and last provides a ``daily panic report`` for the users to record and assess their panic episodes, depressive moods, and dysfunctional thoughts, which it then displays as a graph. The results of the one-month trial showed that the severity of panic disorder symptoms were significantly decreased, and that interactive CBT using the chatbot is both feasible and effective for patients with panic disorder. \textit{Todaki} was later repurposed for Attention Deficit Disorder (ADD) -- also known as Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) -- which is very common to depression and anxiety patients. Similarly, psychoeducation sessions and CBT were used to mitigate associated psychiatric symptoms, such as inattention, impulsivity, and hyperactivity through behavioral change (time, space, and work management), emotional control, and daily conversation checks on concentration and anxiety \cite{adhd2021}. \textit{Todaki}'s addition was the use of social dialogue skills, such as empathy and reflection, to create a therapeutic bond with the users. The study's results showed a decrease in inattention/memory problems, and hyperactivity/restlessness, and verified the effective use of interactive CBT via a chatbot.

On schizophrenia management, an early 2010 voice-based relational agent named \textit{Laura} was used in a month-long pilot study to promote long-term anti-psychotic medication adherence and walking as a physical activity. Medication non-adherence in schizophrenia approaches 50\% the first year, and 75\% the second year with ensuing relapse estimated at an annual rate of 46\%. Interestingly, \textit{Laura} was designed to establish a warm interpersonal relationship with the patient by projecting emotions and using supporting language, also known as ``therapeutic alliance`` \cite{schizophrenia2010}. According to \citeauthor{bickmoreetalrealtionalfordepression2010} relational agents are ``animated conversational agents designed to establish trust and therapeutic alliance with users over time [...] through displays of empathy, close proximity, more frequent eye gaze and attentiveness, social dialogue and humor``. This empathetic relationship has been shown to improve symptoms during psychotherapy \cite{aipsychotherapy2019}. \textit{Laura} interacted with patients once a day to check and remind adherence to their medication and exercise regimen, set new exercise goals for the patient to work towards before the next conversation, displayed the self-reported adherence charts, and also offered tips and education, e.g. on managing medication side effects, strategies for preempting forgetting, or overcoming common barriers to physical activity. \textit{Laura} kept track of medication refills and reminded patients of pharmacy visits, and also prompted the patient to call their nurse in critical circumstances, e.g. when the patient had reported self-harming thoughts. Towards the end of the intervention she taught techniques for self monitoring. Laura exhibited empathy and trust through verbal (calling user by name, using first person plural grammar types, empathetic exchanges, social chat, humor, and reciprocal self-disclosure) and non-verbal behavior (hand gestures, head nods, eye gaze movements, eye brow raises, posture shifts, proxemics, and facial displays of emotion), but without the stigma and anxiety of a face-to-face human encounter. Interaction with users was simplified: they responded not with free-form speech, but by selecting an utterance from a dynamically-updated multiple-choice input menu. This design choice was made to accommodate individuals with a wide range of computer literacy levels and voice qualities, and also to safeguard against potentially dangerous effects of misunderstanding ambiguous utterances about medical topics. \textit{Laura} scored high on acceptance, usability, and self-reported adherence. 

On PTSD, as mentioned earlier in Section \ref{Diagnosis}, two voice-based interventions for user self-assessment have been studied so far for military service members. Both included embodied virtual human interviewers that used anonymity as well as rapport-building behaviors, and have resulted in higher and more accurate disclosure of symptoms of PTSD \cite{SimCoach2016, mentalhealthbarriers2017}.

Substance Use Disorders (SUDs) are considered a common form of mental health disorder \cite{alcoholism2016}. On alcohol abuse, as already mentioned in Section \ref{PublicHealth}, a text-based chatbot was studied in 2016 that provides education on drinking responsibly, performs a standardized screening, and offers expert advice and links to organizations for seeking medical assistance \cite{alcoholism2016}. On smoking addiction, an intervention was studied in 2016, in which a text-based prototype chatbot was developed to divert smokers from smoke cravings during the critical first three weeks of a cessation program \cite{smokingcessation2017}. The bot offered immediate motivating comments, multimedia content, games, and connection with the other participants, thus helping to keep the around 7,000 participants from relapsing. In a subsequent edition of the program, the chatbot's successor tracked the users' smoking behavior (location, time, mood, desire intensity, etc.), and offered the statistics to raise the patients' awareness on their smoking patterns; then, it provided more personalized support with possible substitution and avoidance strategies \cite{smokingcessation2019}. 

However, even though clinical AI has shown great potential in performing tasks related to psychotherapy (i.e. assessment and delivery of therapy), launched products and prototypes are not yet sophisticated enough to fully replace psychotherapists \footnote{'Toward Responsible Development and Evaluation of LLMs in Psychotherapy' (Policy Brief). Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence (HAI), Stanford University. June 13, 2024. Accessed on October 30, 2024. \url{https://hai.stanford.edu/toward-responsible-development-and-evaluation-llms-psychotherapy}}. Moreover, concerns about patient safety have already been raised. Major voice-based conversational agents have been shown to provide no information on SUD treatment according to a 2019 study \cite{noblesetal2020addiction}. The study examined responses to people seeking help to quit an addiction (alcohol, opioids, marijuana, tobacco) by 5 popular virtual assistants: Amazon’s \textit{Alexa}, Apple’s \textit{Siri}, \textit{Google Assistant}, Microsoft’s \textit{Cortana}, and Samsung’s \textit{Bixby}. Almost no agent gave a singular and coherent response (with the exception of \textit{Google Assistant} that directed to a mobile smoking cessation app), and their responses often led to confusion or even promoted substance retailers! A similar study conducted in October 2017 concluded that there is still ``room for improvement`` for smartphone digital assistants on the topic of smoking cessation advice \cite{smokecessation2018}. The researchers compared \textit{Google Assistant} and \textit{Siri} on quality of information, that is information taken from health agencies with medical expertise, such as hospitals, specialist clinics, and universities, versus low-quality sources, such as magazines or individual blogs. \textit{Google Assistant} returned expert content only about half the time, whereas \textit{Siri} just one in four times. A 2015-2016 research examined the responses on mental health issues and interpersonal violence of 4 commercial, general-purpose conversational assistants: Apple's \textit{Siri}, Google's \textit{Google Now}, Samsung's \textit{S Voice}, and Microsoft's \textit{Cortana}. Victims hesitate to report such sensitive problems for fear of stigmatization and retaliation, and this is why effective interventions are sometimes implemented too late or not at all. The agents were evaluated on their ability to flag a human utterance as concerning (e.g. “I want to commit suicide.”, “I was beaten up by my husband.”) and refer them to the appropriate helpline, for instance for suicide prevention, depression, domestic violence, or sexual abuse. The responses, though, were assessed as inconsistent and incomplete regarding recognition of a personal crisis, referrals to resources, or even showing respect \cite{mentalhealthandviolence2016}. Last, the PTSD agent was found to be lacking in recognizing trigger-worthy user responses, therefore missing cases that need referral to emergency services \cite{SimCoach2016}. Therefore, for conversational agents to be able to offer guidance during personal crises, more collaboration among software developers and health authorities is necessary.


\subsection{Providing communication therapy} \label{communication}
Neurological conditions, such as autism, Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease, stroke, and dementia, lead to difficulties in cognitive and motor skills, social interaction, speech, and behavior. Conversational agents can help these patients to increase their socialization skills by providing cognitive stimulation and repetitive practise in social dialogue.

\textit{Harlie (Human And Robot Language Interaction Experiment)} was a 2016 smartphone voice- and text-based application designed to hold a large variety of conversations with people suffering from neurological conditions, such as Parkinson's disease and dementia \cite{harlie2016}. These populations have difficulty initiating and managing a conversation, which leads to distress and gradual social exclusion. \textit{Harlie's} job is to place a random call daily to monitor for challenges these people may be facing while analyzing their language articulation, vocabulary range, and the length of mid-sentence pauses. The system logs this information and then passes it, with consent, to health and research teams to aid further research and provide more targeted therapy \cite{csiro_chatbots}. Ultimately, \textit{Harlie} provides conversational practise, feedback as well as non-judging companionship.

For Alzheimer’s disease (AD) -- the most common form of dementia -- the standard form of therapy is regular (even daily) reminiscence therapy, which involves conversations between patient and caregiver that evoke the patient's old past experiences. The goal is to focus on the patient’s long-term memory, which is easily accessible during the moderate AD phase compared to their short-term memory, in order to stabilize the patient's psychological state, stimulate their memory and cognitive functions, and increase their self-confidence and sociability \cite{dementiatherapy2019}. To this end, a voice-based conversational agent has been proposed to provide daily, personalized dialogs for each patient based on their life history and lifestyle information \cite{dementiatherapy2020}. Also, as recently as in 2025 a purely voice-based chatbot was presented, \textit{Remi}, which combined a continuously growing memory context through summarizing past sessions, as well as empathetic engagement and emotional support \footnote{ Chatbot: Empowering Medical Communication using AI, NLP Healthcare Summit 2025, Accessed on April 6, 2025. \url{https://app.swapcard.com/event/healthcare-nlp-summit-2025/planning/UGxhbm5pbmdfMjU3Njg5Mg==}}. However, concerns have been raised since 2009 regarding the interaction behavior of older users, who typically treat spoken dialog systems like real humans \cite{oldsocialstyle2009}. Thus, they become very sociable and chatty, putting a strain onto the speech recognition and natural language understanding components of a voice companion.

For patients of Autism Spectrum Disorders (ASD), who lack communication skills, social interaction difficulties and even isolation are common problems \cite{ecaforautism2017}. Social skills training (SST), which is the recommended treatment, is typically carried out by a therapist, but it can be automated by a virtual agent. The benefits of using such agents include the augmentation of social interactions, through the establishment of a relationship between the user and the infinitely patient SST coach \cite{FitTrack2003}. In a 2017 study, a voice-based embodied agent was developed to coach the narrative skill, explaining its goals and demonstrating it for the patients to imitate afterwards in a role-play \cite{ecaforautism2017}. Interestingly, the system recorded an audiovisual video of the patient and could then analyze the speech (i.e. F0 variation, amplitude, voice quality, pauses, words per minute, words over six letters, and fillers) and facial expressions (i.e. horizontal and vertical head pose, smiling ratio) to provide feedback on strengths and weaknesses. Also, the agent set minor homework challenges for users to finish over the week, and report on the experience in the following session. The results showed improvement and maintenance of narrative skills, and demonstrate that virtual SST coaches can be used either as an automated social skills trainer or as a supplement to human-based SST.

\subsection{Automating communication and documentation tasks for medical staff}
Dialogue systems can assist clinical staff by saving them time and energy from executing certain repetitive and monotonous tasks in the inpatient setting, such as answering common patient questions, remembering and following lengthy protocols, and documenting the patients' health status, among other standardized tasks of the clinical and nursing workflow. By digitally supporting these tasks via a mobile device (e.g. a smartphone), medical staff can save time, increase their productivity, reduce the risk for errors, and ultimately focus on physically interacting with their patients. Also, doctors' and nurses' job satisfaction can be enhanced, and the so-called ``clinician burnout`` , already mentioned in Section \ref{Clinicians with burnout}, can be mitigated. Since patient satisfaction and treatment outcomes are connected to the medical staff's workplace contentment \cite{delgadillo2018}, improving the latter should improve the former, too. 

On patient question answering, generative AI chatbots, like ChatGPT, are already being used by doctors for drafting medical reports or sending letters and emails \footnote{Stermer C., 'AI ChatGPT for Medicine', TikTok (video), December 12, 2022. Accessed on September 7, 2024. https://www.tiktok.com/@tiktokrheumdok/video/7176340747170467114}, thus saving time and effort. Also, since conversational agents can empower patients, as explained earlier, less strain and fewer demands are placed on physicians. AI-powered patient portals may soon become the “primary contact point” for health information, as AI-curated answers of patients' medical questions can save doctors time, provide quick and accurate answers, and free up resources, eliminating unnecessary clinical visits \cite{ayersetall2023redditempathy, llmsmodernmedicine2023}. Specifically, the commercial ChatGPT chatbot (version GPT-3.5) was used in a 2023 study to produce answers to patients' health questions publicly posted in a social media forum \cite{ayersetall2023redditempathy}. Its responses were afterwards compared with the actual responses given by verified health care professionals on that forum. The evaluators -- who were themselves licensed health care professionals -- rated both the quality of information and the empathy provided to be considerably higher in chatbot responses than physician responses, and ultimately preferred the AI-generated responses 4 to 1. This study suggests that AI chatbots could help physicians in messaging patients by providing a first draft that a professional or even a support member of staff would then proofread and edit (\cite{ayersetall2023redditempathy}). A practical example is Mayo Clinic's nurse staff who have been using generative AI-drafted responses to patient messages since early 2023. This initiative saves the staff around 30 seconds of time spent per message (1 min. and 20 sec. compared to 1 min. and 50 sec. when writing from scratch) with the added advantage that the final messages receive the patients' appreciation, as they are more conversational, friendly, and detailed than the ones that nurses used to write on their own \footnote{``Gen AI Saves Nurses Time by Drafting Responses to Patient Messages``, Epic (blog), 04 March 2024, Accessed July 29, 2025. \url{https://www.epicshare.org/share-and-learn/mayo-ai-message-responses}}. However, a 2024 study conducted by Nigam Shah, Professor of Medicine, Stanford School of Medicine, and UC San Diego, examining LLMs answering patients' questions in text format, showed that the time spent by doctors reviewing and editing the AI-drafted responses has remained the same (i.e. there were no productivity gains), and only their cognitive load has reduced (i.e. only efficiency has increased) \footnote{AI Transforms Health Care | Artificial Intelligence: The Future of Medicine \& Health Care Is Here, Stanford Health Care, YouTube (video), Accessed on September 30, 2024. \url{https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wD1qn2i3Wb4}}. What is more, another 2024 study published by the medical schools at Harvard, Yale, and Wisconsin indicated an alarming 7\% possibility of significant patient safety risks when AI-generated responses were left unedited or not reviewed critically by clinicians \footnote{``Patients Not Told AI Drafted Messages From Their Doctors``, Douglas Mark, April 07 2025, Accessed July 29, 2025.\url{https://www.mhaonline.com/blog/ai-messages-from-doctors}}.

On memorizing and following lengthy protocols, regarding the care of patients with major depression, a relational embodied nurse agent, \textit{Elizabeth}, was developed in a 2010 study to orally provide information and counseling in an empathetic fashion to hospitalized patients about their discharge plan, following a standardized hospital discharge protocol designed to reduce readmissions \cite{bickmoreetalrealtionalfordepression2010}. The protocol involves a human nurse spending half an hour with each patient, ensuring they understand their post-discharge self-care regimen: medications, follow-up appointments, exercise and diet regimens, and pending lab tests. While this protocol is effective, the additional nursing time required makes it impractical to implement, motivating the development of the relational nurse agent to perform this task. The nurse agent produces a personalized ``After Hospital Care Plan`` and reviews it linearly with the patient, while also engaging in social dialogue and giving comprehension tests. Interaction with the patient was chosen to take place through selecting options on a touch screen, due to the noisy environment of the hospital room, as well as non-English accents. At the end of the interaction, any unresolved patient questions are displayed for a human nurse to follow up with the patient. All participants rated the agent very high on satisfaction and ease of use and, notably, most preferred receiving their discharge information from the agent compared to the hospital doctors or nurses. It can be concluded that agents with relational behavior could provide an effective medium for delivering discharge education as well as comfort to patients with depression. 

On following guidelines, a recent example is the project by ``Penda Health`` company in Kenya in February 2024, where an application was introduced to offer advice on diagnosis and treatment to nurses as well as doctors handling a wide range of primary care cases \cite{korom2024}. The way it works is that clinicians enter the patient's diagnosis, and then receive some local guidelines and recommendations for treatment as options to either accept or reject. Notably, the people responsible for implementing the solution kept in mind the 'alert fatigue' described in the Subsection \ref{Mistrust}, and made the tool completely optional. By not forcing its use for every patient visit, ultimately clinicians' rating for the AI consultant was 99.6\% positive.
On AD and related dementias (ADRD), apart from therapy already mentioned in Section \ref{communication}, having proper eating behavior and nutrition can also slow the illness's progression and cognitive decline \cite{dietforAD2020}. In 2020 the \textit{ADRD-DietAssistant} was developed to educate caregivers -- who are typically non-professionals, e.g. spouses, family members or friends of the patient -- on healthy diet (i.e. which food items it should include and how to cook meals), guidelines, and constraints for people with ADRD, interacting in free-form, natural language. The assistant provided personalized answers and recommendations based on the caregiver's and patient's profile, such as on the caregiver's time limitations, financial ability, and education level as well as on the patient's age, stage, food preferences and allergies.

On documenting patients' progress either in real-time or post-hoc, a variety of products that assist with note-taking and entering structured information directly onto Electronic Health Records (EHRs) by voice alone are already in use by clinicians to streamline administrative processes. All of them promise to decrease the ``documentation burden``, as discussed earlier in Section \ref{Clinicians with burnout}. For instance, \textit{Suki} is an AI-powered voice assistant that allows physicians to dictate clinical notes in free-form speech, suggesting notes after a patient visit, and recommending ICD codes (International Classification of Diseases) for diagnoses. \textit{Suki} achieves high accuracy, is available for 99 medical specialties and 80 languages, and is HIPAA compliant \footnote{Suki (website). Accessed on November 5, 2024. \url{https://www.suki.ai/}}. Specifically for nurses, the \textit{NurseBrain} smartphone and web app assists nurses with real-time documentation and note generation, AI suggestions for patient management during bedside care (i.e. actions to take and observations to make), general organization of the nursing workflow, and facilitation of handover communication. The app features voice dictation for recording information about patients on the fly, which it then shows in one place (e.g. name, age, sex, room, current situation, background, assessment, recommendation, etc.). \textit{Nursebrain} keeps the nurse organized with to-do checklists and customized reminders of tasks, their priority, due dates and times, and also provides mood check-ins for nurses. Last, it easily hands off the patients' information to the next nurse in shift by a simple QR scan \footnote{Nursebrain (website). Accessed on November 7, 2024. \url{https://nursebrain.com/}}. Another popular mobile app for nurses is \textit{Rover}, which assists with secure barcoded medication administration (BCMA), that is the right dosages, dates, and times for the right patient. The app features the interesting functionality of taking advantage of the in-built scanner and camera of the device, with which the nurse can scan the barcode on the patient's bracelet for verification and for pulling up patient lists and charts, or scan the barcode of the medication for confirmation, or photograph a wound for documentation \footnote{'How Can the Epic Rover App Transform Your Clinical Workflows?'. SuretySystems. February 2, 2023. Accessed on November 7, 2024. \url{https://www.suretysystems.com/insights/how-can-epic-rover-app-transform-clinical-workflows/}}. \textit{Know My Patient} is another nurse-facing mobile app used for gathering and accessing clinical (i.e. physiological and psychosocial status) and personal (e.g. the patient’s behaviors and responses to treatments, schedules at home, preferences for care during hospitalization) information about multiple patients, thus providing instant, and consistent, individualized care \footnote{Know My Patient, Nightingale Apps (website). Accessed on November 7, 2024. \url{https://nightingaleapps.com/know-my-patient/}}. More recently, \textit{ChatEHR} was released in 2025 by Stanford Medicine to allow clinicians to interact with their patients' actual medical records, boasting accurate and secure responses \footnote{"Clinicians can ‘chat’ with medical records through new AI software, ChatEHR", June 05, 2025, Stanford Medicine News Center (blog). Accessed on June 15, 2025. \url{https://med.stanford.edu/news/all-news/2025/06/chatehr.html}}. Even though still in pilot stage, the chatbot assists 33 Stanford Hospital clinicians in their medical practice by answering questions about a patient’s medical history as well as summarizing patient's charts. 


\subsection{Limitations of CAs in healthcare}
Using AI technology in healthcare -- a domain with very high stakes -- brings forth a range of concerns about its design and deployment, mainly falling into two categories: safety and effectiveness, and privacy. Especially the use of AI in mental healthcare, must balance not only these, but also sensitivity to patients' psychology, cultural adaptability, and avoidance of reinforcement of stigmas \cite{opportunitiesrisks2024}.

The safety and effectiveness category comprises two subcategories: being safe as well as effective. Being safe means that the technology should work to prevent, and should not actively promote, dangerous or unhealthy human behaviors, e.g. self-harm or substance abuse. Being effective means that is should decrease the symptoms of the disorder or the clinical issue being targeted, and increase well-being and quality of life \cite{READI2024}. Human supervision of AI-generated content is necessary to ensure accuracy, and avoid false, biased, or harmful information \cite{ethicallimitations_2020, aipsychotherapy2019}. A systematic literature review carried out in February 2018 on general-purpose conversational agents that accept any unconstrained natural language input (i.e. spoken and typed) for health-related issues, concluded that the studies screened lacked adequate evidence of patient safety and effectiveness \cite{CAhealthcarereview2018}. Regarding the same assistants, as analyzed in Section \ref{SafetyRisks}, other studies have reported inappropriate responses to both critical and non-critical medical questions \cite{healthprompts2020}, as well as significant NLU misrecognitions of drug names and even of whole health-related queries due to non-native English accents \cite{mouth2019, safetyrisks2018}. Specifically for mental health, as mentioned in Section \ref{MentalHealthRisks}, these commercial agents have been found to offer no coherent or helpful advice to patients with SUDs \cite{noblesetal2020addiction} and to patients seeking smoking cessation guidance \cite{smokecessation2018}. They have also been found to respond incompletely and disrespectfully to mental health as well as physical health emergencies \cite{mentalhealthandviolence2016, SimCoach2016}. As expressed concisely by \citeauthor{aipsychotherapy2019}: ``If conversational AI isn’t safe it should not be used, and if it isn’t trusted, it won’t be.`` 

Regarding privacy, the blurring of the laws, norms, and ethics of confidentiality and data sharing is a great challenge \cite{aipsychotherapy2019}. We know that it is forbidden for human clinicians to disclose identifiable patient information as well as a patient's sensitive -- and potentially stigmatizing -- information, e.g. sexual history, trauma, substance abuse, suicidal ideation, etc., otherwise the clinician will face legal consequences. Therefore, similar accountability must be applied to clinical AI agents involved in interactions with patients, and other related third-parties, especially since such agents have an almost infinite capacity to remember and share conversations. 

As a last note, it is crucial to responsibly develop and continuously evaluate clinical conversational agents to ensure that their large-scale deployment is guided by the principles of clinical effectiveness, safety, and explainability \cite{chaGPTpassesUSMLE2023, stade2024}.
